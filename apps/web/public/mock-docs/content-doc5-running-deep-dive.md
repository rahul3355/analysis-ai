# Running Footwear Category Deep Dive -- JD Sports UK H1 2026

**Document Type:** Category Analysis
**Period:** February 2026 to July 2026 (H1 FY2027)
**Author:** Category Manager, Footwear
**Date:** 15 August 2026
**Classification:** CONFIDENTIAL -- INTERNAL USE ONLY

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The running footwear category generated GBP 226 million in revenue during H1 2026, representing 104.6% of the GBP 216 million plan and growth of 15.0% versus the prior year period.

Running footwear is the fastest growing subcategory within footwear, growing at 15.0% year on year compared to total footwear growth of 4.2%. Running now accounts for 17.0% of total footwear revenue, up from 15.5% in the prior year.

The growth is driven by three factors. Technical innovation in midsole foam and carbon plate technology is driving replacement cycles. The running boom that began during the pandemic has sustained, with more people running regularly than pre-2020. New brands, particularly Hoka and On Running, are attracting new customers into the category.

Gross margin for the category was 44.0%, 0.5 percentage points below the footwear average of 44.5%. The margin pressure comes from higher promotional activity on premium technical product, where markdown depth is deeper than in lifestyle footwear.

## 2. Brand Share Analysis

Eight brands contribute over 99% of running footwear revenue in JD UK.

Nike is the market leader with GBP 90 million in revenue, representing 39.8% of the category. Growth was 8.0% versus the prior year. The Pegasus franchise is the cornerstone of the Nike running range, with the Pegasus 41 generating GBP 8.2 million in H1. Gross margin on Nike running is 45.0%, slightly above the category average.

Adidas is second with GBP 49 million, representing 21.7% of the category. Growth was 6.0% versus the prior year. The Ultraboost Light remains the bestselling Adidas running model, but the franchise is facing increased competition from newer products.

ASICS is third with GBP 32 million, representing 14.2% of the category. Growth was 24.0% versus the prior year, making ASICS the second fastest growing brand in the category. The Gel Kayano 31 and Novablast 4 were both top 10 SKUs. ASICS has strong brand equity in the serious runner segment and benefits from high customer satisfaction and repeat purchase rates.

Hoka is fourth with GBP 20 million, representing 8.8% of the category. Growth was 52.0% versus the prior year, making Hoka the fastest growing brand in the category. The Clifton 9 and Bondi 8 were the top sellers. Hoka is still relatively under-distributed in JD, currently in 85 stores, and there is significant opportunity to expand. Supply constraints from Hoka have been the limiting factor rather than demand.

New Balance is fifth with GBP 17.5 million, representing 7.7% of the category. Growth was 18.0% versus the prior year. The Fresh Foam X 1080v13 is the top seller, and the brand has strong momentum in the premium segment.

On Running is sixth with GBP 13.5 million, representing 6.0% of the category. Growth was 35.0% versus the prior year. The Cloudstratus 3 and Cloudmonster are the top sellers. On has very strong brand awareness among younger runners and has successfully positioned itself as a premium technical brand.

Saucony is seventh with GBP 2.5 million, representing 1.1% of the category. Growth was 8.0%. The Triumph and Ride franchises have a loyal but small customer base.

Brooks is eighth with GBP 1.5 million, representing 0.7% of the category. Growth was 5.0%. Brooks has a strong following in the serious runner community but limited brand awareness among the broader JD customer base.

## 3. Price Architecture

Running footwear is segmented into four price tiers based on recommended retail price.

The Core tier, priced at GBP 60 to GBP 89, accounts for 22.0% of units sold and 15.0% of revenue. Models include the Nike Revolution and Adidas Duramo. Gross margin is 42.0%. This tier is important for attracting new and casual runners but has limited profitability.

The Mid tier, priced at GBP 90 to GBP 129, accounts for 30.0% of units and 28.0% of revenue. Models include the Nike Pegasus and ASICS Cumulus. Gross margin is 44.0%. This is the volume heartland of the category.

The Premium tier, priced at GBP 130 to GBP 179, accounts for 32.0% of units and 38.0% of revenue. Models include the ASICS Nimbus, Hoka Clifton, and On Cloudstratus. Gross margin is 45.5%. This is the most profitable tier and the one with the strongest growth trajectory.

The Super Premium tier, priced at GBP 180 and above, accounts for 16.0% of units and 19.0% of revenue. Models include the Nike Alphafly, Hoka Bondi, and On Cloudboom. Gross margin is 47.0%. This tier has the highest margins but also the highest markdown risk due to its relatively low volume and specialist customer base.

## 4. Top 10 SKUs

The top 10 running footwear SKUs by revenue for H1 were as follows.

Nike Pegasus 41 (GBP 120 RRP) was the number one SKU with revenue of GBP 8.2 million across approximately 68,000 pairs. The Pegasus franchise is the most consistent seller in the running category, appealing to both casual and serious runners. The mid-tier price point of GBP 120 positions it at the accessible end of the premium segment.

ASICS Gel Kayano 31 (GBP 155 RRP) was number two with revenue of GBP 5.6 million across approximately 36,000 pairs. The Gel Kayano is the market leader in the stability running shoe segment and has a fiercely loyal customer base.

Hoka Clifton 9 (GBP 130 RRP) was number three with revenue of GBP 4.9 million across approximately 38,000 pairs. The Clifton is Hoka's bestselling model and the entry point to the brand for many new customers.

Adidas Ultraboost Light (GBP 140 RRP) was number four with revenue of GBP 4.6 million across approximately 33,000 pairs. The Ultraboost franchise has been a strong performer for Adidas but faces increasing competition from Hoka and On.

Nike Vomero 17 (GBP 140 RRP) was number five with revenue of GBP 4.1 million across approximately 29,000 pairs. The Vomero is Nike's premium cushioned running shoe and appeals to runners seeking maximum comfort.

New Balance Fresh Foam X 1080v13 (GBP 145 RRP) was number six with revenue of GBP 3.8 million across approximately 26,000 pairs. New Balance continues to gain share in the premium running segment.

ASICS Novablast 4 (GBP 135 RRP) was number seven with revenue of GBP 3.1 million across approximately 23,000 pairs. The Novablast is popular with runners who want a lively, responsive feel for faster training.

On Cloudstratus 3 (GBP 165 RRP) was number eight with revenue of GBP 3.2 million across approximately 19,000 pairs. On has strong brand appeal and the Cloudstratus is its premium daily trainer.

Hoka Bondi 8 (GBP 150 RRP) was number nine with revenue of GBP 2.9 million across approximately 19,000 pairs. The Bondi is Hoka's maximum cushion shoe and appeals to runners prioritising comfort.

Nike Invincible 3 (GBP 160 RRP) was number ten with revenue of GBP 2.7 million across approximately 17,000 pairs. The Invincible is Nike's maximum cushion offering and competes directly with the Hoka Bondi.

## 5. Size Curve Analysis

The size curve for running footwear shows significant regional variation.

Across the UK as a whole, the top selling sizes are UK 10 at 14.2% of volume, UK 9 at 13.8%, UK 11 at 12.1%, and UK 8 at 11.5%. The slowest selling sizes are UK 6 at 3.2% and UK 14 at 1.8%.

In Scotland, the curve shifts significantly towards larger sizes. UK 10 represents 16.1% of volume and UK 11 represents 14.2%. Combined, UK 10 and UK 11 account for over 30% of running shoe sales in Scotland, compared to 26.3% nationally.

In London and the South East, the curve shifts smaller. UK 9 represents 15.5% of volume and UK 8 represents 13.2%. The demographic difference between the regions is consistent across all brands and price points.

The implication for stock allocation is clear. Scottish stores require significantly more large-size stock than the national average allocation. The H1 performance issues in Scotland were partly attributed to an oversupply of UK 8 and UK 9 sizes and an undersupply of UK 10 and UK 11, which suppressed conversion rates in the running category.

## 6. Margin Analysis

The running footwear category achieved a gross margin of 44.0% in H1, 0.5 percentage points below the footwear average.

Markdown erosion is the primary driver of margin pressure. The running category has a full-price sell-through rate of 68%, meaning that 32% of units are sold at a discount. For comparison, lifestyle footwear has a full-price sell-through rate of 82%.

The average discount depth on promoted running shoes is 24%, slightly deeper than the footwear average of 21%. Premium and super premium tiers require deeper discounts to clear, as the customer base is smaller and more specialist.

Margin by brand varies significantly. ASICS achieves the highest running margin at 46.0%, driven by strong full-price demand and a loyal customer base. Hoka achieves 44.5%, which is lower than the brand average due to higher promotional activity as JD and Hoka work to build the brand's awareness. Nike running achieves 45.0% and Adidas running achieves 43.0%.

## 7. Competitor Pricing

Price positioning versus competitors is an important factor in the running category performance.

The Nike Pegasus 41 is priced at GBP 120 at JD, GBP 120 at Nike.com, and GBP 115 at Sports Direct. JD is at parity with Nike direct but 4% above Sports Direct. The price difference is acceptable given JD's superior service and return proposition.

The ASICS Gel Kayano 31 is priced at GBP 155 at JD and GBP 160 at ASICS.com. JD has a 3% price advantage over the brand's own channel. Sports Direct does not stock this model, giving JD a unique position in the multibrand retail channel.

The Hoka Clifton 9 is priced at GBP 130 at JD and GBP 130 at Hoka.com, at parity. Foot Locker also stocks this model at GBP 130. The limited availability of Hoka product across the market means price competition is less intense than for Nike products.

## 8. Stock Health

Running footwear stock at the end of H1 was GBP 78 million at retail value, representing 8.5 weeks of cover. The target is 7.5 weeks.

Aged stock over 90 days represents 7.5% of running stock value, better than the total footwear aged stock figure of 9.8%. The running category benefits from faster stock turns driven by consistent demand throughout the year, rather than the seasonal peaks and troughs seen in lifestyle footwear.

The top 5 SKUs account for 24% of running stock value. This concentration risk means that any stock issues with these key models have an outsized impact on the category. The Pegasus 41 alone accounts for approximately 6% of running stock.

## 9. Recommendations

Five strategic recommendations are proposed based on this analysis.

First, expand Hoka distribution from 85 stores to 200 stores. Hoka is growing at 52% year on year and supply constraints are the only barrier to growth. Negotiating increased allocation from Hoka should be the top priority for the buying team in Q3.

Second, increase the premium tier share from 38% to 42% of running revenue. The premium tier at GBP 130 to GBP 179 delivers the highest margins and the strongest growth. Expanding the range by adding more models from ASICS, Hoka, and On will capture demand that is currently being lost to specialist running retailers.

Third, rebalance Scottish store size curves to increase UK 10 and UK 11 allocations by 20% and reduce UK 8 and UK 9 allocations by 15%. This change alone could improve running category conversion in Scottish stores by an estimated 3% to 5%.

Fourth, implement a controlled price promotion on the Nike Pegasus 41 in January to clear aged stock ahead of the Pegasus 42 launch. A 25% discount for two weeks would clear an estimated 70% of aged stock while maintaining margin above 40%.

Fifth, negotiate a 12-month exclusivity agreement with Hoka for the UK multibrand retail channel. Hoka's rapid growth and JD's distribution strength make this a mutually beneficial opportunity. Exclusivity would protect JD's margin on Hoka product and prevent price competition from other retailers.
